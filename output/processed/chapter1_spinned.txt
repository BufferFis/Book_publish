The CANOE BUILDER D ICK standing on a ledge of coral cast his eyes to the South. Behind him the breakers of the outer sea thundered and the spindrift scattered on the wind; before him stretched an ocean calm as the lake, infinite, blue, and flown about by the fishing gulls—the lagoon of Karolin. Then, yesterday, from the northern sea in a strange boat and with Katafa, the girl who had been blown to sea years ago when out fishing, this strange new figure had come, sent by the gods, so the women said, to be their chief and ruler. “Taori,” said little Tari, “who are you?” (é kamina tai) Could Dick have answered? Katafa to Marua, the storms that swept the island and the sea, and all that was forgotten as though consumed in the great blaze of tropic light that bathed the arc of the ocean, whose light was engulfed in a great fire of light, which was bathing the islands and seas. The men of Palm Tree had no canoes, but they would build them, and as surely they could see the far mirror blaze of Karolin lagoon in the sky, just as he had seen it. It might be a very long time yet, though they had built them. Then they saw their landing on this beach, yesterday and the women and children swarming round him, the man whom they considered sent by the gods to be their chief and leader. Palm Tree was beginning to live, to bloom, and to come to life, a fig tree was starting to grow in Karolin’s vineyards and ripening in the vines of the grove of palm trees that grew on the shore of Lake Mead, where it flourished for thousands of years. Then he heard the yells of the Melanesians mad with rum stolen from their schooner, and which they had burnt. And there, there before them lay the scene of The Tragedy, that lovely picture which showed nothing of demons that still in habited it. Dick gazed on this loveliness, which was yet a threat and warning, his nostrils expanded and his eyes grew dark with hate.
